完整原文｜定位词与证据原位标注

彩色 marker = 题干定位词；粗下划线 + Q 标签 = 作答证据。

P11What would you do with $590m? This is now a question for Gloria MacKenzie, an 84-year-old widow who recently emerged from her small, tin-roofed house in Florida to collect the biggest undivided lottery jackpot in history. 2If she hopes her new-found fortune will yield lasting feelings of fulfilment, she could do worse than read Happy Money by Elizabeth Dunn and Michael Norton.

P23These two academics use an array of behavioral research to show that the most rewarding ways to spend money can be counterintuitive. 4Fantasies of great wealth often involve visions of fancy cars and extravagant homes. 5Yet satisfaction with these material purchases wears off fairly quickly. 6What was once exciting and new becomes old-hat; regret creeps in. 7It is far better to spend money on experiences, say Ms Dunn and Mr Norton, like interesting trips, unique meals or even going to the cinema. 8These purchases often become more valuable with time — as stories or memories — particularly if they involve feeling more connected to others.

P39This slim volume is packed with tips to help wage slaves as well as lottery winners get the most “happiness bang for your buck.” 10It seems most people would be better off if they could shorten their commutes to work, spend more time with friends and family and less of it watching television (something the average American spends a whopping two months a year doing, and is hardly jollier for it). 11Buying gifts or giving to charity is often more pleasurable than purchasing things for oneself, and luxuries are most enjoyable when they are consumed sparingly. 12This is apparently the reason McDonald’s restricts the availability of its popular McRib — a marketing trick that has turned the pork sandwich into an object of obsession.

P4Q24 段落定位Q25 全文主旨13Readers of Happy Money are clearly a privileged lot, anxious about fulfilment, not hunger. 14Money may not quite buy happiness, but people in wealthier countries are generally happier than those in poor ones. 15Yet the link between feeling good and spending money on others can be seen among rich and poor people around the world, and scarcity enhances the pleasure of most things for most people. 16Not everyone will agree with the authors’ policy ideas, which range from mandating more holiday time to reducing tax incentives for American homebuyers. 17But most people will come away from this book believing it was money well spent.

定位闭环补全层

This text mainly discusses how to ________. If she hopes her new-found fortune will yield lasting feelings of fulfilment... / the most rewarding ways to spend money...

逐题证据链解析

题干关键词 → 原文定位 → 顺序切片 → 证据 → 替换 → 排除。

Q21细节题
答案 B

According to Dunn and Norton, which of the following is the most rewarding purchase?

  • [A]A big house.
  • [B]A special tour.
  • [C]A stylish car.
  • [D]A rich meal.
关键词题干 Dunn and Norton → 原文 Ms Dunn and Mr Norton；purchase → spend money；special tour → interesting trips / experiences。
定位定位到 P2，先排除 fancy cars / extravagant homes，再看 Yet 后的正向结论。
证据It is far better to spend money on experiences... like interesting trips, unique meals or even going to the cinema.
替换A special tour = interesting trips = spending money on experiences。

干扰项反证｜先 steelman，再回到原文边界

[A]big house 属于 extravagant homes，原文说满足感很快消退。
[C]stylish car 对应 fancy cars，同样属于被否定的物质购买。
[D]rich meal 看似接近 meals，但原文是 unique meals，且四项中 special tour 最稳对应 experiences / interesting trips。

  • [C]sympathetic

证据the average American spends a whopping two months a year doing, and is hardly jollier for it.

完整原文｜定位词与证据原位标注

彩色 marker = 题干定位词；粗下划线 + Q 标签 = 作答证据。

P1Q26 段落定位1An article in Scientific American has pointed out that empirical research says that, actually, you think you’re more beautiful than you are. 2We have a deep-seated need to feel good about ourselves and we naturally employ a number of self-enhancing strategies to achieve this. 3Social psychologists have amassed oceans of research into what they call the “above average effect,” or “illusory superiority,” and shown that, for example, 70% of us rate ourselves as above average in leadership, 93% in driving and 85% at getting on well with others — all obviously statistical impossibilities.

P24We rose-tint our memories and put ourselves into self-affirming situations. 5We become defensive when criticised, and apply negative stereotypes to others to boost our own esteem. 6We stalk around thinking we’re hot stuff.

P37Psychologist and behavioural scientist Nicholas Epley oversaw a key study into self-enhancement and attractiveness. 8Rather than have people simply rate their beauty compared with others, he asked them to identify an original photograph of themselves from a lineup including versions that had been altered to appear more and less attractive. 9Visual recognition, reads the study, is “an automatic psychological process, occurring rapidly and intuitively with little or no apparent conscious deliberation.” 10If the subjects quickly chose a falsely flattering image — which most did — they genuinely believed it was really how they looked.

P411Epley found no significant gender difference in responses. 12Nor was there any evidence that those who self-enhanced the most were doing so to make up for profound insecurities. 13In fact, those who thought that the images higher up the attractiveness scale were real directly corresponded with those who showed other markers for having higher self-esteem. 14“I don’t think the findings that we have are any evidence of personal delusion,” says Epley. 15“It’s a reflection simply of people generally thinking well of themselves.” 16If you are depressed, you won’t be self-enhancing.

P5Q29 词义定位17Knowing the results of Epley’s study, it makes sense that many people hate photographs of themselves viscerally — on one level, they don’t even recognise the person in the picture as themselves. 18Facebook, therefore, is a self-enhancer’s paradise, where people can share only the most flattering photos, the cream of their wit, style, beauty, intellect and lifestyles. 19It’s not that people’s profiles are dishonest, says Catalina Toma of Wisconsin-Madison University, “but they portray an idealised version of themselves.”

完整原文｜定位词与证据原位标注

彩色 marker = 题干定位词；粗下划线 + Q 标签 = 作答证据。

P1Q31 段落定位1The concept of man versus machine is at least as old as the industrial revolution, but this phenomenon tends to be most acutely felt during economic downturns and fragile recoveries. 2And yet, it would be a mistake to think we are right now simply experiencing the painful side of a boom and bust cycle. 3Certain jobs have gone away for good, outmoded by machines. 4Since technology has such an insatiable appetite for eating up human jobs, this phenomenon will continue to restructure our economy in ways we cannot immediately foresee.

P25When there is rapid improvement in the price and performance of technology, jobs that were once thought to be immune from automation suddenly become threatened. 6This argument has attracted a lot of attention, via the success of the book Race Against the Machine, by Erik Brynjolfsson and Andrew McAfee, who both hail from MIT’s Center for Digital Business.

P37This is a powerful argument, and a scary one. 8And yet, John Hagel, author of The Power of Pull and other books, says Brynjolfsson and McAfee miss the reason why these jobs are so vulnerable to technology in the first place.

P49Hagel says we have designed jobs in the U.S. that tend to be “tightly scripted” and “highly standardized” ones that leave no room for “individual initiative or creativity”. 10In short, these are the types of jobs that machines can perform much better than human beings. 11That is how we have put a giant target sign on the backs of American workers, Hagel says.

P512It’s time to reinvent the formula for how work is conducted, since we are still relying on a very 20th century notion of work, Hagel says. 13In our rapidly changing economy, we more than ever need people in the workplace who can take initiative and exercise their imagination “to respond to unexpected events.” 14That is not something machines are good at. 15They are designed to perform very predictable activities.

P6Q34 段落定位Q35 主旨定位16As Hagel notes, Brynjolfsson and McAfee indeed touched on this point in their book. 17We need to reframe race against the machine as race with the machine. 18In other words, we need to look at the ways in which machines can augment human labor rather than replace it. 19So then the problem is not really about technology, but rather, “how do we innovate our institutions and our work practices?”

定位闭环补全层

most appropriate title
