完整原文｜定位词与证据原位标注

彩色 marker = 题干定位词；粗下划线 + Q 标签 = 作答证据。

P11It’s true that high-school coding classes aren’t essential for learning computer science in college. 2Students without experience can catch up after a few introductory courses, said Tom Cortina, the assistant dean at Carnegie Mellon’s School of Computer Science.

P23However, Cortina said, early exposure is beneficial. 4When younger kids learn computer science, they learn that it’s not just a confusing, endless string of letters and numbers — but a tool to build apps, or create artwork, or test hypotheses. 5It’s not as hard for them to transform their thought processes as it is for older students. 6Breaking down problems into bite-sized chunks and using code to solve them becomes normal. 7Giving more children this training could increase the number of people interested in the field and help fill the jobs gap, Cortina said.

P38Students also benefit from learning something about coding before they get to college, where introductory computer-science classes are packed to the brim, which can drive the less-experienced or -determined students away.

P49The Flatiron School, where people pay to learn programming, started as one of the many coding bootcamps that’s become popular for adults looking for a career change. 10The high-schoolers get the same curriculum, but “we try to gear lessons toward things they’re interested in,” said Victoria Friedman, an instructor. 11For instance, one of the apps the students are developing suggests movies based on your mood.

P512The students in the Flatiron class probably won’t drop out of high school and build the next Facebook. 13Programming languages have a quick turnover, so the “Ruby on Rails” language they learned may not even be relevant by the time they enter the job market. 14But the skills they learn — how to think logically through a problem and organize the results — apply to any coding language, said Deborah Seehorn, an education consultant for the state of North Carolina.

P6Q24 段落定位Q25 词义定位15Indeed, the Flatiron students might not go into IT at all. 16But creating a future army of coders is not the sole purpose of the classes. 17These kids are going to be surrounded by computers — in their pockets, in their offices, in their homes — for the rest of their lives. 18The younger they learn how computers think, how to coax the machine into producing what they want — the earlier they learn that they have the power to do that — the better.

答案 B

Cortina holds that early exposure to computer science makes it easier to ________.transform their thought processes

  • [A]complete future job training
  • [B]remodel the way of thinking
  • [C]formulate logical hypotheses
  • [D]perfect artwork production

干扰项反证｜先 steelman，再回到原文边界

完整原文｜定位词与证据原位标注

彩色 marker = 题干定位词；粗下划线 + Q 标签 = 作答证据。

P11Biologists estimate that as many as 2 million lesser prairie chickens — a kind of bird living on stretching grasslands — once lent red to the often grey landscape of the midwestern and southwestern United States. But just some 22,000 birds remain today, occupying about 16% of the species’ historic range.

P22The crash was a major reason the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service (USFWS) decided to formally list the bird as threatened. 3“The lesser prairie chicken is in a desperate situation,” said USFWS Director Daniel Ashe. 4Some environmentalists, however, were disappointed. 5They had pushed the agency to designate the bird as “endangered,” a status that gives federal officials greater regulatory power to crack down on threats. 6But Ashe and others argued that the “threatened” tag gave the federal government flexibility to try out new, potentially less confrontational conservation approaches. 7In particular, they called for forging closer collaborations with western state governments, which are often uneasy with federal action, and with the private landowners who control an estimated 95% of the prairie chicken’s habitat.

P3Q28 段落定位8Under the plan, for example, the agency said it would not prosecute landowners or businesses that unintentionally kill, harm, or disturb the bird, as long as they had signed a range-wide management plan to restore prairie chicken habitat. 9Negotiated by USFWS and the states, the plan requires individuals and businesses that damage habitat as part of their operations to pay into a fund to replace every acre destroyed with 2 new acres of suitable habitat. 10The fund will also be used to compensate landowners who set aside habitat. 11USFWS also set an interim goal of restoring prairie chicken populations to an annual average of 67,000 birds over the next 10 years. 12And it gives the Western Association of Fish and Wildlife Agencies (WAFWA), a coalition of state agencies, the job of monitoring progress. Overall, the idea is to let “states remain in the driver’s seat” for managing the species, Ashe said.

P413Not everyone buys the win-win rhetoric. 14Some Congress members are trying to block the plan, and at least a dozen industry groups, four states, and three environmental groups are challenging it in federal court. 15Not surprisingly, industry groups and states generally argue it goes too far; environmentalists say it doesn’t go far enough. 16“The federal government is giving responsibility for managing the bird to the same industries that are pushing it to extinction,” says biologist Jay Lininger.

完整原文｜定位词与证据原位标注

彩色 marker = 题干定位词；粗下划线 + Q 标签 = 作答证据。

P11That everyone’s too busy these days is a cliché. But one specific complaint is made especially mournfully: There’s never any time to read.

P22What makes the problem thornier is that the usual time-management techniques don’t seem sufficient. 3The web’s full of articles offering tips on making time to read: “Give up TV” or “Carry a book with you at all times.” 4But in my experience, using such methods to free up the odd 30 minutes doesn’t work. 5Sit down to read and the flywheel of work-related thoughts keeps spinning — or else you’re so exhausted that a challenging book’s the last thing you need. 6The modern mind, Tim Parks, a novelist and critic, writes, “is overwhelmingly inclined toward communication... It is not simply that one is interrupted; it is that one is actually inclined to interruption.” 7Deep reading requires not just time, but a special kind of time which can’t be obtained merely by becoming more efficient.

P38In fact, “becoming more efficient” is part of the problem. 9Thinking of time as a resource to be maximised means you approach it instrumentally, judging any given moment as well spent only in so far as it advances progress toward some goal. 10Immersive reading, by contrast, depends on being willing to risk inefficiency, goallessness, even time-wasting. 11Try to slot it in as a to-do list item and you’ll manage only goal-focused reading — useful, sometimes, but not the most fulfilling kind. 12“The future comes at us like empty bottles along an unstoppable and nearly infinite conveyor belt,” writes Gary Eberle in his book Sacred Time, and “we feel a pressure to fill these different-sized bottles (days, hours, minutes) as they pass, for if they get by without being filled, we will have wasted them.” 13No mind-set could be worse for losing yourself in a book.

P414So what does work? 15Perhaps surprisingly, scheduling regular times for reading. 16You’d think this might fuel the efficiency mind-set, but in fact, Eberle notes, such ritualistic behaviour helps us “step outside time’s flow” into “soul time.” 17You could limit distractions by reading only physical books, or on single-purpose e-readers. 18“Carry a book with you at all times” can actually work, too — providing you dip in often enough, so that reading becomes the default state from which you temporarily surface to take care of business, before dropping back down. 19On a really good day, it no longer feels as if you’re “making time to read,” but just reading, and making time for everything else.

定位闭环补全层

The best title for this text could be ________. There’s never any time to read. / So what does work? Perhaps surprisingly, scheduling regular times for reading.

定位闭环补全层

One cross-generation mark of a successful life is ________.

逐题证据链解析

题干关键词 → 原文定位 → 顺序切片 → 证据 → 替换 → 排除。

Q36细节题
答案 B

One cross-generation mark of a successful life is ________. One cross-generation mark of a successful life is ________.having children

  • [A]trying out different lifestyles
  • [B]having a family with children
  • [C]working beyond retirement age
  • [D]setting up a profitable business
关键词cross-generation mark → Across generational lines；successful life 原词定位。
定位P2 列举各代共同珍视的成功人生里程碑，包括结婚、生子、拥有住房、退休。
证据Americans continue to prize many of the same traditional milestones... including getting married, having children...
替换having a family with children = getting married + having children。

干扰项反证｜先 steelman，再回到原文边界

[A]different paths/lifestyles 是代际差异，不是共同标志。
[C]retiring in their sixties 不是 beyond retirement age。
[D]profitable business 未出现。
Q37段落细节题
答案 C

It can be learned from Paragraph 3 that young people tend to ________.

  • [A]favor a slower life pace
  • [B]hold an occupation longer
  • [C]attach importance to pre-marital finance
  • [D]give priority to childcare outside the home
关键词Paragraph 3 段首锚点；young people 原文复现。
定位P3 明确说年轻人认为结婚或生子前应有经济保障。
证据couples should be financially secure before getting married or having children.
替换pre-marital finance = financially secure before getting married。

干扰项反证｜先 steelman，再回到原文边界

[A]原文是 faster pace of life，反向。
[B]原文是 regularly changing jobs，反向。
[D]two parents working outside the home 是家庭照料观点，不是优先“外部 childcare”。
Q38细节题
答案 D

The priorities and expectations defined by the young will ________.spread through virtually all aspects of American life

  • [A]become increasingly clear
  • [B]focus on materialistic issues
  • [C]depend largely on political preferences
  • [D]reach almost all aspects of American life

定位P4 说这些优先事项和期待会扩散到美国生活几乎所有方面。
证据priorities and expectations that will increasingly spread through virtually all aspects of American life...

[A]clear 未出现。
[B]从消费到住房到政治，不是单纯物质问题。
[C]politics 只是受影响领域之一，不是决定因素。
Q39细节题
答案 D

Both young and old agree that ________.harder for young people today to get started in life

  • [A]good-paying jobs are less available
  • [B]the old made more life achievements
  • [C]housing loans today are easy to obtain
  • [D]getting established is harder for the young
关键词young and old 原词定位；agree/converge → one key point。
定位P5 直接说两组多数认为年轻人今天更难起步。
证据Overwhelming majorities of both groups said they believe it is harder for young people today to get started in life...
替换getting established = get started in life / reach signpost achievements。

干扰项反证｜先 steelman，再回到原文边界

[C]finding affordable housing 更难，非贷款容易。

  • [A]He found a dream job after graduating from college.
  • [B]His parents believe working steadily is a must for success.
  • [C]His parents’ good life has little to do with a college degree.
  • [D]He thinks his job as a technician quite challenging.

证据his parents could provide a comfortable life... even though neither had completed college...
替换good life has little to do with a college degree = comfortable life even though neither completed college。
