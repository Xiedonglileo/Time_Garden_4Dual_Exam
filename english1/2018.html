完整原文｜定位词与证据原位标注

彩色加粗 marker = 题干关键词或原文同义定位表达；粗下划线 + Q 标签 = 作答证据。主旨题证据可分布在多个段落。

P1 S1Among the annoying challenges facing the middle class is one that will probably go unmentioned in the next presidential campaign: What happens when the robots come for their jobs?

P2 S1Don't dismiss that possibility entirely. S2About half of U.S. jobs are at high risk of being automated, according to a University of Oxford study, with the middle class disproportionately squeezed. S3Lower-income jobs like gardening or day care don't appeal to robots. S4But many middle-class occupations—trucking, financial advice, software engineering—have aroused their interest, or soon will. S5The rich own the robots, so they will be fine.

P3 S1This isn't to be alarmist. S2Optimists point out that technological upheaval has benefited workers in the past. S3The Industrial Revolution didn't go so well for Luddites whose jobs were displaced by mechanized looms, but it eventually raised living standards and created more jobs than it destroyed. S4Likewise, automation should eventually boost productivity, stimulate demand by driving down prices, and free workers from hard, boring work. S5But in the medium term, middle-class workers may need a lot of help adjusting.

P4 S1The first step, as Erik Brynjolfsson and Andrew McAfee argue in The Second Machine Age, should be rethinking education and job training. S2Curriculums—from grammar school to college—should evolve to focus less on memorizing facts and more on creativity and complex communication. S3Vocational schools should do a better job of fostering problem-solving skills and helping students work alongside robots. S4Online education can supplement the traditional kind. S5It could make extra training and instruction affordable. S6Professionals trying to acquire new skills will be able to do so without going into debt.

P5S1The challenge of coping with automation underlines the need for the U.S. to revive its fading business dynamism: Starting new companies must be made easier. S2In previous eras of drastic technological change, entrepreneurs smoothed the transition by dreaming up ways to combine labor and machines. S3The best uses of 3D printers and virtual reality haven't been invented yet. S4The U.S. needs the new companies that will invent them.

P6 S1Finally, because automation threatens to widen the gap between capital income and labor income, taxes and the safety net will have to be rethought. S2Taxes on low-wage labor need to be cut, and wage subsidies such as the earned income tax credit should be expanded: This would boost incomes, encourage work, reward companies for job creation, and reduce inequality.

P7 S1Technology will improve society in ways big and small over the next few years, yet this will be little comfort to those who find their lives and careers upended by automation. S2Destroying the machines that are coming for our jobs would be nuts. S3But policies to help workers adapt will be indispensable.

Q21细节定位题
答案 D

Who will be most threatened by automation?

  • ALeading politicians.
  • BLow-wage laborers.
  • CRobot owners.
  • DMiddle-class workers.
关键词题干 most threatened by automation → 原文 high risk / disproportionately squeezed / middle-class occupations。
定位首题按顺序看开头两段；P2 连续比较低收入者、中产和富人。
证据P2 说中产阶级被不成比例地挤压，且许多中产职业已经或即将引起机器人的兴趣。
替换most threatened = disproportionately squeezed；automation = robots / automated。

干扰项反证

Apresidential campaign 只是引入话题，不是受威胁人群。
B原文说 low-income jobs don't appeal to robots，方向相反。
C富人拥有机器人，so they will be fine。

Which of the following best represents the author's view?

  • BOptimists' opinions on new tech find little support.

  • DNegative consequences of new tech can be avoided.

证据中期中产需要大量帮助调整；帮助工人适应的政策不可缺少。

完整原文｜定位词与证据原位标注

彩色加粗 marker = 题干关键词或原文同义定位表达；粗下划线 + Q 标签 = 作答证据。主旨题证据可分布在多个段落。

P1 S1A new survey by Harvard University finds more than two-thirds of young Americans disapprove of President Trump's use of Twitter. S2The implication is that Millennials prefer news from the White House to be filtered through other sources, not a president's social media platform.

P2 S1Most Americans rely on social media to check daily headlines. S2Yet as distrust has risen toward all media, people may be starting to beef up their media literacy skills. S3Such a trend is badly needed. S4During the 2016 presidential campaign, nearly a quarter of web content shared by Twitter users in the politically critical state of Michigan was fake news, according to the University of Oxford. S5And a survey conducted for BuzzFeed News found 44 percent of Facebook users rarely or never trust news from the media giant.

P3 S1Young people who are digital natives are indeed becoming more skillful at separating fact from fiction in cyberspace. S2A Knight Foundation focus-group survey of young people between ages 14 and 24 found they use “distributed trust” to verify stories. S3They cross-check sources and prefer news from different perspectives—especially those that are open about any bias. S4“Many young people assume a great deal of personal responsibility for educating themselves and actively seeking out opposing viewpoints,” the survey concluded.

P4S1Such active research can have another effect. S2A 2014 survey conducted in Australia, Britain, and the United States by the University of Wisconsin-Madison found that young people's reliance on social media led to greater political engagement.

P5 S1Social media allows users to experience news events more intimately and immediately while also permitting them to re-share news as a projection of their values and interests. S2This forces users to be more conscious of their role in passing along information. S3A survey by Barna research group found the top reason given by Americans for the fake news phenomenon is “reader error,” more so than made-up stories or factual mistakes in reporting. S4About a third say the problem of fake news lies in “misinterpretation or exaggeration of actual news” via social media. S5In other words, the choice to share news on social media may be the heart of the issue. S6“This indicates there is a real personal responsibility in counteracting this problem,” says Roxanne Stone, editor in chief at Barna Group.

P6S1So when young people are critical of an over-tweeting president, they reveal a mental discipline in thinking skills—and in their choices on when to share on social media.

完整原文｜定位词与证据原位标注

彩色加粗 marker = 题干关键词或原文同义定位表达；粗下划线 + Q 标签 = 作答证据。主旨题证据可分布在多个段落。

P1 S1Any fair-minded assessment of the dangers of the deal between Britain's National Health Service (NHS) and DeepMind must start by acknowledging that both sides mean well. S2DeepMind is one of the leading artificial intelligence (AI) companies in the world. S3The potential of this work applied to healthcare is very great, but it could also lead to further concentration of power in the tech giants. S4It is against that background that the information commissioner, Elizabeth Denham, has issued her damning verdict against the Royal Free hospital trust under the NHS, which handed over to DeepMind the records of 1.6 million patients in 2015 on the basis of a vague agreement which took far too little account of the patients' rights and their expectations of privacy.

P2 S1DeepMind has almost apologized. S2The NHS trust has mended its ways. S3Further arrangements—and there may be many—between the NHS and DeepMind will be carefully scrutinised to ensure that all necessary permissions have been asked of patients and all unnecessary data has been cleaned. S4There are lessons about informed patient consent to learn. S5But privacy is not the only angle in this case and not even the most important. S6Ms Denham chose to concentrate the blame on the NHS trust, since under existing law it “controlled” the data and DeepMind merely “processed” it. S7But this distinction misses the point that it is processing and aggregation, not the mere possession of bits, that gives the data value.

P3S1The great question is who should benefit from the analysis of all the data that our lives now generate. S2Privacy law builds on the concept of damage to an individual from identifiable knowledge about them. S3That misses the way the surveillance economy works. S4The data of an individual there gains its value only when it is compared with the data of countless millions more.

P4 S1The use of privacy law to curb the tech giants in this instance feels slightly maladapted. S2This practice does not address the real worry. S3It is not enough to say that the algorithms DeepMind develops will benefit patients and save lives. S4What matters is that they will belong to a private monopoly which developed them using public resources. S5If software promises to save lives on the scale that drugs now can, big data may be expected to behave as a big pharma has done.

P5S1We are still at the beginning of this revolution and small choices now may turn out to have gigantic consequences later. S2A long struggle will be needed to avoid a future of digital feudalism. S3Ms Denham's report is a welcome start.
