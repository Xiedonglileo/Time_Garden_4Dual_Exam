完整原文｜定位词与证据原位标注

彩色加粗 marker = 题干关键词或原文同义定位表达；粗下划线 + Q 标签 = 作答证据。

P1 S1How can Britain’s train operators possibly justify yet another increase to rail passenger fares? S2It has become a grimly reliable annual ritual: every January the cost of travelling by train rises, imposing a significant extra burden on those who have no option but to use the rail network to get to work or otherwise. S3This year’s rise, an average of 2.7 per cent, may be a fraction lower than last year’s, but it is still well above the official Consumer Price Index (CPI) measure of inflation.

P2 S1Successive governments have permitted such increases on the grounds that the cost of investing in and running the rail network should be borne by those who use it, rather than the general taxpayer. S2Why, the argument goes, should a car-driving pensioner from Lincolnshire have to subsidise the daily commute of a stockbroker from Surrey? S3Equally, there is a sense that the travails of commuters in the South East, many of whom will face among the biggest rises, have received too much attention compared to those who must endure the relatively poor infrastructure of the Midlands and the North.

P3 S1However, over the past 12 months, those commuters have also experienced some of the worst rail strikes in years. S2It is all very well train operators trumpeting the improvements they are making to the network, but passengers should be able to expect a basic level of service for the substantial sums they are now paying to travel. S3The responsibility for the latest wave of strikes rests on the unions. S4However, there is a strong case that those who have been worst affected by industrial action should receive compensation for the disruption they have suffered.

P4 S1The Government has pledged to change the law to introduce a minimum service requirement so that, even when strikes occur, services can continue to operate. S2This should form part of a wider package of measures to address the long-running problems on Britain’s railways. S3Yes, more investment is needed, but passengers will not be willing to pay more indefinitely if they must also endure cramped, unreliable services, interrupted by regular chaos when timetables are changed, or planned maintenance is managed incompetently. S4The threat of nationalisation may have been seen off for now, but it will return with a vengeance if the justified anger of passengers is not addressed in short order.

Q21观点态度题
正确答案 D

The author holds that this year’s increase in rail passenger fares

  • Awill ease train operators’ burden.
  • Bhas kept pace with inflation.
  • Cis a big surprise to commuters.
  • Dremains an unreasonable measure.
关键词题干关键词 this year’s increase / rail passenger fares → 原文 yet another increase / This year’s rise。
定位开篇 P1 直接评价涨价：反问“possibly justify”，再说明它加重无选择乘客的负担，并且高于 CPI。
证据“How can ... justify yet another increase”不是中性提问，而是质疑；“significant extra burden”和“well above CPI”共同指向不合理。
替换unreasonable measure = hard to justify + extra burden + above CPI；this year’s increase = This year’s rise。

干扰项反证

[A]原文没有说涨价能减轻运营商负担；讨论对象是乘客负担。
[B]原文说 well above CPI，不是“跟通胀同步”。
[C]annual ritual / grimly reliable 表明并不令人意外。

完整原文｜定位词与证据原位标注

彩色加粗 marker = 题干关键词或原文同义定位表达；粗下划线 + Q 标签 = 作答证据。

P1S1Last year marked the third year in a row that Indonesia’s bleak rate of deforestation has slowed in pace. S2One reason for the turnaround may be the country’s antipoverty program.

P2 S1In 2007, Indonesia started phasing in a program that gives money to its poorest residents under certain conditions, such as requiring people to keep kids in school or get regular medical care. S2Called conditional cash transfers or CCTs, these social assistance programs are designed to reduce inequality and break the cycle of poverty. S3They’re already used in dozens of countries worldwide. S4In Indonesia, the program has provided enough food and medicine to substantially reduce severe growth problems among children.

P3S1But CCT programs don’t generally consider effects on the environment. S2In fact, poverty alleviation and environmental protection are often viewed as conflicting goals, says Paul Ferraro, an economist at Johns Hopkins University.

P4 S1That’s because economic growth can be correlated with environmental degradation, while protecting the environment is sometimes correlated with greater poverty. S2However, those correlations don’t prove cause and effect. S3The only previous study analyzing causality, based on an area in Mexico that had instituted CCTs, supported the traditional view. S4There, as people got more money, some of them may have cleared more land for cattle to raise for meat, Ferraro says.

P5 S1Such programs do not have to negatively affect the environment, though. S2Ferraro wanted to see if Indonesia’s poverty-alleviation program was affecting deforestation. S3Indonesia has the third-largest area of tropical forest in the world and one of the highest deforestation rates.

P6 S1Ferraro analyzed satellite data showing annual forest loss from 2008 to 2012—including during Indonesia’s phase-in of the antipoverty program—in 7,468 forested villages across 15 provinces. S2“We see that the program is associated with a 30 percent reduction in deforestation,” Ferraro says.

P7S1That’s likely because the rural poor are using the money as makeshift insurance policies against inclement weather, Ferraro says. S2Typically, if rains are delayed, people may clear land to plant more rice to supplement their harvests. S3With the CCTs, individuals instead can use the money to supplement their harvests.

P8 S1Whether this research translates elsewhere is anybody’s guess. S2Ferraro suggests results may transfer to other parts of Asia, due to commonalities such as the importance of growing rice and market access. S3And regardless of transferability, the study shows that what’s good for people may also be good for the environment. S4Even if this program didn’t reduce poverty, Ferraro says, “the value of the avoided deforestation just for carbon dioxide emissions alone is more than the program costs.”

完整原文｜定位词与证据原位标注

彩色加粗 marker = 题干关键词或原文同义定位表达；粗下划线 + Q 标签 = 作答证据。

P1 S1As a historian who’s always searching for the text or the image that makes us re-evaluate the past, I’ve become preoccupied with looking for photographs that show our Victorian ancestors smiling (what better way to shatter the image of 19th-century prudery?). S2I’ve found quite a few, and—since I started posting them on Twitter—they have been causing quite a stir. S3People have been surprised to see evidence that Victorians had fun and could, and did, laugh. S4They are noting that the Victorians suddenly seem to become more human as the hundred-or-so years that separate us fade away through our common experience of laughter.

P2 S1Of course, I need to concede that my collection of “Smiling Victorians” makes up only a tiny percentage of the vast catalogue of photographic portraiture created between 1840 and 1900, the majority of which show sitters posing miserably and stiffly in front of painted backdrops, or staring absently into the middle distance. S2How do we explain this trend?

P3 S1During the 1840s and 1850s, in the early days of photography, exposure times were notoriously long: the daguerreotype photographic method (producing an image on a silvered copper plate) could take several minutes to complete, resulting in blurred images as sitters shifted position or adjusted their limbs. S2The thought of holding a fixed grin as the camera performed its magical duties was too much to contemplate, and so a non-committal blank stare became the norm.

P4 S1But exposure times were much quicker by the 1880s, and the introduction of the Box Brownie and other portable cameras meant that, though slow by today’s digital standards, the exposure was almost instantaneous. S2Spontaneous smiles were relatively easy to capture by the 1890s, so we must look elsewhere for an explanation of why Victorians still hesitated to smile.

P5 S1One explanation might be the loss of dignity displayed through a cheesy grin. S2“Nature gave us lips to conceal our teeth,” ran one popular Victorian saying, alluding to the fact that before the birth of proper dentistry, mouths were often in a shocking state of hygiene. S3A flashing set of healthy and clean, regular “pearly whites” was a rare sight in Victorian society, the preserve of the super-rich (and even then, dental hygiene was not guaranteed).

P6 S1A toothy grin (especially when there were gaps or blackened teeth) lacked class: drunks, tramps and music hall performers might gurn and grin with a smile as wide as Lewis Carroll’s gum-exposing Cheshire Cat, but it was not a becoming look for properly bred persons. S2Even Mark Twain, a man who enjoyed a hearty laugh, said that when it came to photographic portraits there could be “nothing more damning than a silly, foolish smile fixed forever”.
