完整原文｜定位词与证据原位标注

彩色加粗 marker = 题干关键词或原文同义定位表达；粗下划线 + Q 标签 = 作答证据。

P1 S1First two hours, now three hours — this is how far in advance authorities are recommending people show up to catch a domestic flight, at least at some major U.S. airports with increasingly massive security lines.

P2 S1Americans are willing to tolerate time-consuming security procedures in return for increased safety. S2The crash of EgyptAir Flight 804, which terrorists may have downed over the Mediterranean Sea, provides another tragic reminder of why. S3But demanding too much of air travelers or providing too little security in return undermines public support for the process. S4And it should: Wasted time is a drag on Americans’ economic and private lives, not to mention infuriating.

P3 S1Last year, the Transportation Security Administration (TSA) found in a secret check that undercover investigators were able to sneak weapons — both fake and real — past airport security nearly every time they tried. S2Enhanced security measures since then, combined with a rise in airline travel due to the improving economy and low oil prices, have resulted in long waits at major airports such as Chicago’s O’Hare International. S3It is not yet clear how much more effective airline security has become — but the lines are obvious.

P4 S1Part of the issue is that the government did not anticipate the steep increase in airline travel, so the TSA is now rushing to get new screeners on the line. S2Part of the issue is that airports have only so much room for screening lanes. S3Another factor may be that more people are trying to overpack their carry-on bags to avoid checked-baggage fees, though the airlines strongly dispute this.

P5 S1There is one step the TSA could take that would not require remodeling airports or rushing to hire: Enroll more people in the PreCheck program. S2PreCheck is supposed to be a win-win for travelers and the TSA. S3Passengers who pass a background check are eligible to use expedited screening lanes. S4This allows the TSA to focus on travelers who are higher risk, saving time for everyone involved. S5The TSA wants to enroll 25 million people in PreCheck.

P6 S1It has not gotten anywhere close to that, and one big reason is sticker shock: S2Passengers must pay $85 every five years to process their background checks. S3Since the beginning, this price tag has been PreCheck’s fatal flaw. S4Upcoming reforms might bring the price to a more reasonable level. S5But Congress should look into doing so directly, by helping to finance PreCheck enrollment or to cut costs in other ways.

P7 S1The TSA cannot continue diverting resources into underused PreCheck lanes while most of the traveling public suffers in unnecessary lines. S2It is long past time to make the program work.

Q21例证题
答案 A

21. The crash of EgyptAir Flight 804 is mentioned to

  • Aexplain America’s tolerance of current security checks.
  • Bstress the urgency to strengthen security worldwide.
  • Chighlight the necessity of upgrading major U.S. airports.
  • Demphasize the importance of privacy protection.
关键词题干 crash of EgyptAir Flight 804 → 原文 crash of EgyptAir Flight 804；其作用要回看前一句 Americans are willing to tolerate... safety。
定位定位 P2。例子前一句给观点，例子本身说明“why”。
证据原文说美国人愿以耗时安检换取安全，坠机事件是“another tragic reminder of why”。
替换tolerance of current security checks = tolerate time-consuming security procedures；crash = reminder of safety risks。

干扰项反证

B原文谈美国国内安检容忍度，不是全球加强安保。
C机场升级不是例子作用。
D隐私保护未成为坠机例证的指向。
Q22细节题
答案 C

22. Which of the following contributes to long waits at major airports?

  • ANew restrictions on carry-on bags.
  • BThe declining efficiency of the TSA.
  • CAn increase in the number of travelers.
  • DFrequent unexpected secret checks.
关键词题干 long waits at major airports → 原文 long waits at major airports；contributes to → combined with / part of the issue。
定位定位 P3-P4，题干原词复现后向前找原因。
证据P3 说 rise in airline travel 与加强安检共同导致 long waits；P4 又说政府未预判 steep increase in airline travel。
替换an increase in the number of travelers = rise / steep increase in airline travel。

干扰项反证

A原文只是说乘客可能多塞随身包，并非“新限制”。
BTSA 效率下降不是明说原因。
Dsecret check 是发现漏洞的背景，不是频繁检查造成排队。

Q25主旨题
答案 C

25. Which of the following would be the best title for the text?

  • ALess Screening for More Safety
  • BPreCheck – a Belated Solution
  • CGetting Stuck in Security Lines
  • DUnderused PreCheck Lanes

完整原文｜定位词与证据原位标注

彩色加粗 marker = 题干关键词或原文同义定位表达；粗下划线 + Q 标签 = 作答证据。

P1 S1“The ancient Hawaiians were astronomers,” wrote Queen Liliuokalani, Hawaii’s last reigning monarch, in 1897. S2Star watchers were among the most esteemed members of Hawaiian society. S3Sadly, all is not well with astronomy in Hawaii today. S4Protests have erupted over construction of the Thirty Meter Telescope (TMT), a giant observatory that promises to revolutionize humanity’s view of the cosmos.

P2 S1At issue is the TMT’s planned location on Mauna Kea, a dormant volcano worshiped by some Hawaiians as the piko, that connects the Hawaiian Islands to the heavens. S2But Mauna Kea is also home to some of the world’s most powerful telescopes. S3Rested in the Pacific Ocean, Mauna Kea’s peak rises above the bulk of our planet’s dense atmosphere, where conditions allow telescopes to obtain images of unsurpassed clarity.

P3 S1Opposition to telescopes on Mauna Kea is nothing new. S2A small but vocal group of Hawaiians and environmentalists have long viewed their presence as disrespect for sacred land and a painful reminder of the occupation of what was once a sovereign nation.

P4 S1Some blame for the current controversy belongs to astronomers. S2In their eagerness to build bigger telescopes, they forgot that science is not the only way of understanding the world. S3They did not always prioritize the protection of Mauna Kea’s fragile ecosystems or its holiness to the island’s inhabitants. S4Hawaiian culture is not a relic of the past; it is a living culture undergoing a renaissance today.

P5 S1Yet science has a cultural history, too, with roots going back to the dawn of civilization. S2The same curiosity to find what lies beyond the horizon that first brought early Polynesians to Hawaii’s shores inspires astronomers today to explore the heavens. S3Calls to disassemble all telescopes on Mauna Kea or to ban future development there ignore the reality that astronomy and Hawaiian culture both seek to answer big questions about who we are, where we come from and where we are going. S4Perhaps that is why we explore the starry skies, as if answering a primal calling to know ourselves and our true ancestral homes.

P6 S1The astronomy community is making compromises to change its use of Mauna Kea. S2The TMT site was chosen to minimize the telescope’s visibility around the island and to avoid archaeological and environmental impact. S3To limit the number of telescopes on Mauna Kea, old ones will be removed at the end of their lifetimes and their sites returned to a natural state. S4There is no reason why everyone cannot be welcomed on Mauna Kea to embrace their cultural heritage and to study the stars.

完整原文｜定位词与证据原位标注

彩色加粗 marker = 题干关键词或原文同义定位表达；粗下划线 + Q 标签 = 作答证据。

P1 S1Robert F. Kennedy once said that a country’s GDP measures “everything except that which makes life worthwhile.” S2With Britain voting to leave the European Union, and GDP already predicted to slow as a result, it is now a timely moment to assess what he was referring to.

P2 S1The question of GDP and its usefulness has annoyed policymakers for over half a century. S2Many argue that it is a flawed concept. S3It measures things that do not matter and misses things that do. S4By most recent measures, the UK’s GDP has been the envy of the Western world, with record low unemployment and high growth figures. S5If everything was going so well, then why did over 17 million people vote for Brexit, despite the warnings about what it could do to their country’s economic prospects?

P3 S1A recent annual study of countries and their ability to convert growth into well-being sheds some light on that question. S2Across the 163 countries measured, the UK is one of the poorest performers in ensuring that economic growth is translated into meaningful improvements for its citizens. S3Rather than just focusing on GDP, over 40 different sets of criteria from health, education and civil society engagement have been measured to get a more rounded assessment of how countries are performing.

P4S1While all of these countries face their own challenges, there are a number of consistent themes. S2Yes, there has been a budding economic recovery since the 2008 global crash, but in key indicators in areas such as health and education, major economies have continued to decline. S3Yet this isn’t the case with all countries. S4Some relatively poor European countries have seen huge improvements across measures including civil society, income equality and the environment.

P5S1This is a lesson that rich countries can learn: S2When GDP is no longer regarded as the sole measure of a country’s success, the world looks very different.

P6 S1So, what Kennedy was referring to was that while GDP has been the most common method for measuring the economic activity of nations, as a measure, it is no longer enough. S2It does not include important factors such as environmental quality or education outcomes — all things that contribute to a person’s sense of well-being.

P7 S1The sharp hit to growth predicted around the world and in the UK could lead to a decline in the everyday services we depend on for our well-being and for growth. S2But policymakers who refocus efforts on improving well-being rather than simply worrying about GDP figures could avoid the forecasted doom and may even see progress.
