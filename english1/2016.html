Q25｜P1-P7｜D

全文围绕时尚业身体理想的误用、法律与伦理规制展开，核心是对行业身体理想的挑战。

完整原文｜定位词与证据原位标注

彩色加粗 marker = 题干关键词或原文同义定位表达；粗下划线 + Q 标签 = 作答证据。主旨题证据可分布在多个段落。

P1 S1France, which prides itself as the global innovator of fashion, has decided its fashion industry has lost an absolute right to define physical beauty for women. S2Its lawmakers gave preliminary approval last week to a law that would make it a crime to employ ultra-thin models on runways. S3The parliament also agreed to ban websites that “incite excessive thinness” by promoting extreme dieting.

P2 S1Such measures have a couple of uplifting motives. S2They suggest beauty should not be defined by looks that end up impinging on health. S3That’s a start. S4And the ban on ultra-thin models seems to go beyond protecting models from starving themselves to death—as some have done. S5It tells the fashion industry that it must take responsibility for the signal it sends women, especially teenage girls, about the social tape-measure they must use to determine their individual worth.

P3S1The bans, if fully enforced, would suggest to women (and many men) that they should not let others be arbiters of their beauty. S2And perhaps faintly, they hint that people should look to intangible qualities like character and intellect rather than dieting their way to size zero or wasp-waist physiques.

P4S1The French measures, however, rely too much on severe punishment to change a culture that still regards beauty as skin-deep—and bone-showing. S2Under the law, using a fashion model that does not meet a government-defined index of body mass could result in a $85,000 fine and six months in prison.

P5 S1The fashion industry knows it has an inherent problem in focusing on material adornment and idealized body types. S2In Denmark, the United States, and a few other countries, it is trying to set voluntary standards for models and fashion images that rely more on peer pressure for enforcement.

P6 S1In contrast to France’s actions, Denmark’s fashion industry agreed last month on rules and sanctions regarding the age, health, and other characteristics of models. S2The newly revised Danish Fashion Ethical Charter clearly states: “We are aware of and take responsibility for the impact the fashion industry has on body ideals, especially on young people.” S3The charter’s main tool of enforcement is to deny access for designers and modeling agencies to Copenhagen Fashion Week (CFW), which is run by the Danish Fashion Institute. S4But in general it relies on a name-and-shame method of compliance.

P7 S1Relying on ethical persuasion rather than law to address the misuse of body ideals may be the best step. S2Even better would be to help elevate notions of beauty beyond the material standards of a particular industry.

According to the first paragraph, what would happen in France?

  • APhysical beauty would be redefined.
  • BNew runways would be constructed.
  • CWebsites about dieting would thrive.
  • DThe fashion industry would decline.

Q22词义猜测题
答案 D

The phrase “impinging on” (Line 2, Para. 2) is closest in meaning to

  • Aindicating the state of.
  • Bheightening the value of.
  • Closing faith in.
  • Ddoing harm to.
关键词impinging on health → looks / health；结合后文 ultra-thin models、starving themselves to death。

Bheighten value 与健康受损语境相反。

完整原文｜定位词与证据原位标注

彩色加粗 marker = 题干关键词或原文同义定位表达；粗下划线 + Q 标签 = 作答证据。主旨题证据可分布在多个段落。

P1 S1For the first time in history more people live in towns than in the country. S2In Britain this has had a curious result. S3While polls show Britons rate “the countryside” alongside the royal family, Shakespeare and the National Health Service (NHS) as what makes them proud of their country, this has limited political support.

P2 S1A century ago Octavia Hill launched the National Trust not to rescue stylish houses but to “save the beauty of natural places for everyone forever.” S2It was specifically to provide city dwellers with spaces for leisure where they could experience “a refreshing air.” S3Hill’s pressure later led to the creation of national parks and green belts. S4They don’t make countryside any more, and every year concrete consumes more of it. It needs constant guardianship.

P3 S1At the next election none of the big parties seem likely to endorse this sentiment. S2The Conservatives’ planning reform explicitly gives rural development priority over conservation, even authorising “off-plan” building where local people might object. S3The concept of sustainable development has been defined as profitable. S4Labour likewise wants to discontinue local planning where councils oppose development. S5The Liberal Democrats are silent. S6Only Ukip, sensing its chance, has sided with those pleading for a more considered approach to using green land. S7Its Campaign to Protect Rural England struck terror into many local Conservative parties.

P4S1The sensible place to build new houses, factories and offices is where people are, in cities and towns where infrastructure is in place. S2The London agent Stirling Ackroyd recently identified enough sites for half a million houses in the London area alone, with no intrusion on green belt. S3What is true of London is even truer of the provinces.

P5 S1The idea that “housing crisis” equals “concreted meadows” is pure lobby talk. S2The issue is not the need for more houses but, as always, where to put them. S3Under lobby pressure, George Osborne favours rural new-build against urban renovation and renewal. S4He favours out-of-town shopping sites against high streets. This is not a free market but a biased one. S5Rural towns and villages have grown and will always grow. They do so best where building sticks to their edges and respects their character. S6We do not ruin urban conservation areas. S7Why ruin rural ones?

P6 S1Development should be planned, not let rip. S2After the Netherlands, Britain is Europe’s most crowded country. S3Half a century of town and country planning has enabled it to retain an enviable rural coherence, while still permitting low-density urban living. S4There is no doubt of the alternative—the corrupted landscapes of southern Portugal, Spain or Ireland. S5Avoiding this rather than promoting it should unite the left and right of the political spectrum.

完整原文｜定位词与证据原位标注

彩色加粗 marker = 题干关键词或原文同义定位表达；粗下划线 + Q 标签 = 作答证据。主旨题证据可分布在多个段落。

P1 S1“There is one and only one social responsibility of business,” wrote Milton Friedman, a Nobel prize-winning economist, “That is, to use its resources and engage in activities designed to increase its profits.” S2But even if you accept Friedman’s premise and regard corporate social responsibility (CSR) policies as a waste of shareholders’ money, things may not be absolutely clear-cut. S3New research suggests that CSR may create monetary value for companies—at least when they are prosecuted for corruption.

P2 S1The largest firms in America and Britain together spend more than $15 billion a year on CSR, according to an estimate by EPG, a consulting firm. S2This could add value to their businesses in three ways. S3First, consumers may take CSR spending as a “signal” that a company’s products are of high quality. S4Second, customers may be willing to buy a company’s products as an indirect way to donate to the good causes it helps. S5And third, through a more diffuse “halo effect,” whereby its good deeds earn it greater consideration from consumers and others.

P3S1Previous studies on CSR have had trouble differentiating these effects because consumers can be affected by all three. S2A recent study attempts to separate them by looking at bribery prosecutions under America’s Foreign Corrupt Practices Act (FCPA). S3It argues that since prosecutors do not consume a company’s products as part of their investigations, they could be influenced only by the halo effect.

P4 S1The study found that, among prosecuted firms, those with the most comprehensive CSR programmes tended to get more lenient penalties. S2Their analysis ruled out the possibility that it was firms’ political influence, rather than their CSR stand, that accounted for the leniency: Companies that contributed more to political campaigns did not receive lower fines.

P5 S1In all, the study concludes that whereas prosecutors should only evaluate a case based on its merits, they do seem to be influenced by a company’s record in CSR. S2“We estimate that either eliminating a substantial labour-rights concern, such as child labour, or increasing corporate giving by about 20% results in fines that generally are 40% lower than the typical punishment for bribing foreign officials,” says one researcher.

P6 S1Researchers admit that their study does not answer the question of how much businesses ought to spend on CSR. S2Nor does it reveal how much companies are banking on the halo effect, rather than the other possible benefits, when they decide their do-gooding policies. S3But at least they have demonstrated that when companies get into trouble with the law, evidence of good character can win them a less costly punishment.
