完整原文｜定位词与证据原位标注

彩色加粗 marker = 题干关键词或原文同义定位表达；粗下划线 + Q 标签 = 作答证据。主旨题证据可分布在多个段落。

P1S1Nearly 2,000 years ago, as the Romans began to pull out of Scotland, they left behind a curious treasure: 10 tons of nails, nearly a million of the things. S2The nail hoard was discovered in 1960 in a four-metre-deep pit covered by two metres of gravel.

P2 S1Why had the Romans buried a million nails? S2The likely explanation is that the withdrawal was rushed, and they didn’t want the local Caledonians getting their hands on 10 tons of weapon-grade iron. S3The Romans buried the nails so deep that they would not be discovered for almost two millennia.

P3 S1Later civilisations would value the skilled blacksmith’s labour in a nail even more than the raw material. S2As Roma Agrawal explains in her new delightful book Nuts and Bolts, early 17th-century Virginians would sometimes burn down their homes if they were planning to relocate. S3This was an attempt to recover the valuable nails, which could be reused after sifting the ashes. S4The idea that one might burn down an entire house just to reclaim the nails underlines how scarce, costly and valuable the simple-seeming technology was.

P4 S1The price of nails fell by 90% between the late 1700s and mid-1900s, as economist Daniel Sichel points out in a research paper. S2According to Sichel, although the falling price of nails was driven partly by cheaper iron and cheaper energy, most of the credit goes to nail manufacturers who simply found more efficient ways to turn steel into nails.

P5 S1Nails themselves have changed over the years, but Sichel studied them because they haven’t changed much. S2Roman lamps and Roman chariots are very different from LED strips and sports cars, but Roman nails are still clearly nails. S3It would be absurd to try to track the changing price of sports cars since 1695, but to ask the same question of nails makes perfect sense.

P6 S1I make no apology for being obsessed by a particular feature of everyday objects: their price. S2I am an economist, after all. S3After writing two books about the history of inventions, one thing I’ve learnt is that while it is the enchantingly sophisticated technologies that get all the hype, it’s the cheap technologies that change the world.

P7 S1The Gutenberg printing press transformed civilisation not by changing the nature of writing but by changing its cost—and it would have achieved little without a parallel collapse in the price of surfaces to write on, thanks to an often-overlooked technology called paper. S2Solar panels had a few niche uses until they became cheap; now they are transforming the global energy system.

Q21细节因果题
答案 D

21. The Romans buried the nails probably for the sake of ________.

  • Asaving them for future use
  • Bkeeping them from rusting
  • Cletting them grow in value
  • Dhiding them from the locals
关键词题干 buried the nails → 原文 buried a million nails / buried the nails so deep；for the sake of → likely explanation。
定位看到 Romans + buried + nails，直接定位 P2。P2 用 why 提出问题，随后给出原因。
证据P2 明说：撤离仓促，罗马人不想让 local Caledonians 拿到 10 吨 weapon-grade iron。
替换hiding them from the locals = didn’t want the local Caledonians getting their hands on them。

干扰项反证

A原文没有说留作将来使用；撤离仓促反而排除“保存备用”。
B没有提到防锈；埋深的目的不是保存质量。
C没有“升值”逻辑；价值判断出现在后文，不是罗马人埋钉的原因。
Q22例证功能题
答案 D

22. The example of early 17th-century Virginians is used to ________.

  • Ahighlight the thriftiness of early American colonists
  • Billustrate the high status of blacksmiths in that period
  • Ccontrast the attitudes of different civilisations towards nails
  • Dshow the preciousness of nail-making technology at that time
关键词题干 early 17th-century Virginians → 原文同词复现；example 用途看例子前后的观点句。
定位定位 P3 中 early 17th-century Virginians；向后读到例子总结句 underlines。
证据P3 说他们为回收 valuable nails 甚至烧房，最后总结该行为说明 simple-seeming technology 稀缺、昂贵、宝贵。
替换preciousness = scarce, costly and valuable；nail-making technology = simple-seeming technology。

干扰项反证

A“节俭”只是表面行为，原文重点不是殖民者品格，而是技术价值。
B前句提到 blacksmith’s labour，但例子总结落在 nails / technology 的稀缺昂贵上。
C没有比较不同文明的态度；罗马与弗吉尼亚只是共同说明铁钉价值。

答案 A

23. What played the major role in lowering the price of nails after the late 1700s?

  • AIncreased productivity.
  • BWider use of new energies.
  • CFiercer market competition.
  • DReduced cost of raw materials.
关键词题干 lowering the price / late 1700s → 原文 price of nails fell / late 1700s；major role → most of the credit。
定位按 price + late 1700s 定位 P4，再抓 most of the credit 判断主因。
证据P4 说价格下降部分来自 cheaper iron and cheaper energy，但 most of the credit 归于制造者更高效地把钢变成钉子。
替换Increased productivity = more efficient ways to turn steel into nails。

干扰项反证

Bcheaper energy 只是 partly，且不是 wider use of new energies。
C全文没有市场竞争信息。
Dcheaper iron 只是 partly，不是 major role。

完整原文｜定位词与证据原位标注

彩色加粗 marker = 题干关键词或原文同义定位表达；粗下划线 + Q 标签 = 作答证据。主旨题证据可分布在多个段落。

P1 S1Parenting tips obtained from hunter-gatherers in Africa may be the key to bringing up more contented children, researchers have suggested. S2The idea is based on studies of communities such as the Kung of Botswana, where each child is cared for by many adults. S3Kung children as young as four will help to look after younger ones and “baby-wearing”, in which infants are carried in slings, is considered the norm.

P2 S1According to Dr Nikhil Chaudhary, an evolutionary anthropologist at Cambridge University, these practices, known as alloparenting, could lead to less anxiety for children and parents.

P3 S1Dr Annie Swanepoel, a child psychiatrist, believes that there are ways to incorporate them into western life. S2In Germany, one scheme has paired an old people’s home with a nursery. S3The residents help to look after the children, an arrangement to alloparenting. S4Another measure could be encouraging friendships between children in different school years to mirror the unsupervised mixed-age playgroups in hunter-gatherer communities.

P4 S1In a paper published in the Journal of Child Psychology and Psychiatry, researchers said that the western nuclear family was a recent invention which broke with evolutionary history. S2This abrupt shift to an “intensive mothering narrative”, which suggests that mothers should manage childcare alone, was likely to have been harmful. S3“Such narratives can lead to maternal exhaustion and have dangerous consequences,” they wrote.

P5S1By contrast, in hunter-gatherer societies adults other than the parents can provide almost half of a child’s care. S2One previous study looked at the Efé people of the Democratic Republic of Congo. S3It found that infants had an average of 14 alloparents a day by the time they were 18 weeks old and were passed between caregivers eight times an hour.

P6 S1Chaudhary said that parents now had less childcare support from family and social networks than during most of humans’ evolutionary history, but introducing additional caregivers could reduce stress and maternal depression, which could have a “knock-on” benefit to a child’s wellbeing. S2An infant born to a hunter-gatherer society could have more than ten caregivers—this contrasts starkly to nursery settings in the UK where regulations call for a ratio of one carer to four children aged two to three.

P7 S1While hunter-gatherer children learnt from observation and imitation in mixed-age playgroups, researchers said that western “instructive teaching”, where pupils are asked to sit still, may contribute to conditions such as attention deficit hyperactivity disorder. S2Chaudhary said that Britain should explore the possibility that older siblings helping their parents “might also enhance their own social development”.

完整原文｜定位词与证据原位标注

彩色加粗 marker = 题干关键词或原文同义定位表达；粗下划线 + Q 标签 = 作答证据。主旨题证据可分布在多个段落。

P1 S1A Polish digital artist who uses classical painting styles to create dreamy fantasy landscapes, Greg Rutkowski has made illustrations for games such as Dungeons & Dragons and Magic: The Gathering. S2And he’s become a sudden hit in the new world of text-to-image AI generation.

P2 S1His distinctive style is now one of the most commonly used prompts in the new open-source AI art generator Stable Diffusion. S2The tool, along with other popular image-generation AI models, allows anyone to create impressive images based on text prompts. S3For example, type in “Wizard with sword and a glowing orb of magic fire fights a fierce dragon Greg Rutkowski,” and the system will produce something that looks not a million miles away from works in Rutkowski’s style.

P3 S1But these open-source programs are built by scraping images from the Internet, often without permission and proper attribution to artists. S2As a result, they are raising tricky questions about ethics and copyright. S3And artists like Rutkowski have had enough.

P4 S1According to the website Lexica, which tracks over 10 million Stable Diffusion images and prompts, Rutkowski’s name has been used as a prompt around 93,000 times. S2Rutkowski was initially surprised but thought it might be a good way to reach new audiences. S3Then he tried searching for his name to see if a piece he had worked on had been published. S4The online search brought back work that had his name attached to it but wasn’t his.

P5S1“It’s been just a month. What about in a year? I probably won’t be able to find my work out there because the Internet will be flooded with AI art,” Rutkowski said. S2“That’s concerning.”

P6S1Other artists besides Rutkowski have been surprised by the apparent popularity of their work in text-to-image generators—and some are now fighting back.

P7S1Karla Ortiz, an illustrator based in San Francisco who found her work in Stable Diffusion’s data set, has been raising awareness about the issues around AI art and copyright. S2Artists say they risk losing income as people start using AI-generated images based on copyrighted material for commercial purposes. S3But it’s also a lot more personal, Ortiz says, arguing that because art is so closely linked to a person, it could raise data protection and privacy problems.

P8 S1“There is a coalition growing within artist industries to figure out how to tackle or mitigate this,” says Ortiz. S2The group is in its early days of mobilization, which could involve pushing for new policies or regulation. S3One suggestion is that AI models could be trained on images in the public domain, and AI companies could forge partnerships with museums and artists, Ortiz says.
