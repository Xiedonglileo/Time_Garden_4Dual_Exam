完整原文｜定位词与证据原位标注

彩色加粗 marker = 题干关键词或原文同义定位表达；粗下划线 + Q 标签 = 作答证据。

P1 S1For thousands of years, donkeys have been critical for propelling human civilizations forward. S2They’ve helped pull wheeled vehicles, carry travelers and move goods across the world. S3But where and when these animals first became intertwined with humans has been a mystery. S4Now, researchers have used genomes of over 200 donkeys to trace their domestication back to a single event around 7,000 years ago in East Africa — about 3,000 years before humans tamed horses. S5The team published their findings in the journal Science this month.

P2 S1“Through their DNA, the animals are telling their history themselves,” co-author Samantha Brooks, an equine researcher at the University of Florida, says in a statement. S2“We usually only get the human’s side of history through written accounts, but of course written history does not always record exactly how something happened. S3Looking at these DNA sequences, we get a biological testimony to the environment these animals lived in and the experiences they survived.”

P3 S1The researchers examined 207 genomes from modern donkeys living in 31 countries across the globe. S2They also looked at genomes from 15 wild equids and 31 earlier donkeys that lived between about 4,000 and 100 years ago. S3The team reconstructed the animals’ evolutionary tree and used computer models to pinpoint the domestication event: when herders in Kenya and the Horn of Africa tamed wild asses. S4They then traced how the animals spread across the rest of the continent into Europe and Asia about 2,500 years later.

P4 S1Though it’s still unclear why the original domestication happened, Science News’ Freda Kreier reports that the event coincided with the Sahara growing larger and drier. S2“Donkeys are champions when it comes to carrying stuff and are good at going through deserts,” co-author Ludovic Orlando, an evolutionary biologist at Paul Sabatier University in France, tells the publication. S3Prehistoric humans may have tamed donkeys to help navigate the expanding Sahara.

P5S1Researchers say these findings could help put donkeys in the spotlight. S2The animals could benefit from more research: Currently, there are no published genomes from donkeys located south of the Equator in Africa. S3But understanding where the animals were first domesticated could guide archaeologists to a narrow region to search for insights about the original tamed donkeys.

P6 S1Not only does human-understanding the equines’ genetic makeup help reveal their contribution to human history, but it also might improve their management in the future, as climate change alters the planet’s environment, write the authors.

逐题证据链解析

每题只保留“关键词—定位—证据—替换—排除”的必要信息。

Q21细节理解题
答案 D

What can be learned about donkeys from paragraph 1?

  1. AThey seemed mysterious to human ancestors.
  2. BThey underwent multiple domestication events.
  3. CThey were vividly portrayed by ancient travelers.
  4. DThey were tamed earlier than horses.
关键词题干关键词 donkeys / paragraph 1；原文定位到 domestication, single event, before humans tamed horses。
定位按题号顺序先看第 1 段，锁定驯化时间比较句。
证据P1 S4 明确说驴的驯化约在 7,000 年前，且“about 3,000 years before humans tamed horses”。
替换tamed earlier than horses = domestication ... before humans tamed horses。

干扰项反证

Amystery 指“驴何时何地与人类交织”是研究谜题，不是祖先觉得驴神秘。
Bsingle event 直接排除 multiple domestication events。
C第 1 段没有 ancient travelers 或 vividly portrayed。
Q22观点理解题
答案 D

What message is conveyed in Brooks’ statement?

  1. AThe earliest habitats of donkeys are hardly traceable.
  2. BIt is increasingly easy to read donkeys’ DNA sequences.
  3. CWritten accounts contain vital clues for donkey research.
  4. DGenetic analysis offers insight into the history of donkeys.
关键词题干关键词 Brooks’ statement；原文定位到 “Through their DNA” 与 “DNA sequences”。
定位Brooks 的引语集中在 P2，反复比较 written accounts 和 DNA sequences。
证据P2 说动物通过 DNA 讲述自己的历史，DNA 序列提供关于生活环境和经历的 biological testimony。
替换Genetic analysis = looking at DNA sequences；offers insight into history = telling their history / biological testimony。

干扰项反证

ABrooks 并未说最早栖息地“难以追踪”，相反 DNA 可提供线索。
B原文没有“越来越容易读取 DNA”的技术难度变化。
CBrooks 说书面历史“不总能准确记录”，不是强调 written accounts 的关键价值。

P1S1There’s no business like show business — but in Los Angeles, it feels like there’s no business at all.

P6 S1As TV and movie productions slowly come back after the writers and actors strikes, they’re nowhere near the volume of 2022 and for the most part, nowhere near Los Angeles. S2Disney has 22 live-action movies slated for production in 2024, with only three to be filmed in California. S3Let’s go on with the show … and make sure the show doesn’t go on without us.

完整原文｜定位词与证据原位标注

彩色加粗 marker = 题干关键词或原文同义定位表达；粗下划线 + Q 标签 = 作答证据。

P1 S1The pioneers of wireless saw it as a gift to all the people. S2Sir John Reith said that it would end “isolation of the spirit” and rejoiced: “It does not matter how many thousand may be listening, there is always enough for others… the genius and the fool, the wealthy and the poor listen simultaneously.”

P2 S1Between two great wars this technological innovation built a new kind of national consciousness. S2Opening this week, a book and exhibition curated by Beatty Rubens at the Bodleian in Oxford records how radio changed everyday life from 1922 to 1939. S3She draws on letters, diaries and fiction, and a 1939 field notebook of verbatim audience research by Winifred Gill.

P3 S1There’s fun in testimonies of people enjoying the sheer newness. S2A cartoon mocks a group failing to converse because they’re all in headphones. S3People report that broadcast music made workmen whistle new tunes. S4A woman says there have been fewer street fights since the arrival of the wireless but also less stopping and “talking on the brush handle”.

P4 S1By and large the wireless was welcome. S2I loved the chap from the Thirties research who found that wireless suddenly offered “a lot of variety … things I thought I’d never be interested in… ice hockey, perhaps”. S3True: for more than 80 pre-digital years, linear speech broadcasting brought the gift of serendipity, random enlivening of a car journey or dull manual task. S4In my own book about radio I recorded how, on one drive: “I caught up with the news, learnt some 17th-century history, and was startlingly educated by an unpretentious programme on the history of the stethoscope.”

P5S1But radio’s enriching serendipity is ebbing. S2With multiple networks and countless podcasts, a smartphone user selects what to hear and when. S3And while it is wonderful to take a walk with anything in your headphones, infinite choice encourages us to shrink into niche interests and sympathetic beliefs.

P6S1A hundred years on from Marconi and Reith, is the art of mere listening endangered? S2Some will say the audiobook boom revives it, though I suppose you can then worry about the decline of reading. S3But inventions shape all of us and it is worth noticing when techno-social habits do change, and asking whether to control them a bit or shield the youngest.

P7 S1Whatever we do, innovation will happen. S2Today we fret about the isolating culture of smartphone-staring and selfie-vanity, but already in 1939 there was that lady regretting how, when all her street got wirelesses, it lost the neighbourly habit of “talking on the brush handle”. S3It’s enough to make a person put down the smartphone and go out front with a yard broom.

What can be learnt about wireless from Reith?

  1. AIt was accessible to everyone.
  2. BIt improved interpersonal relations.
  3. CIt was a miracle of human ingenuity.
  4. DIt led to a new era of isolating culture.
