完整原文｜定位词与证据原位标注

彩色加粗 marker = 题干关键词或原文同义定位表达；粗下划线 + Q 标签 = 作答证据。

P1 S1People often complain that plastics are too durable. S2Water bottles, shopping bags, and other trash litter the planet, from Mount Everest to the Mariana Trench, because plastics are everywhere and don’t break down easily. S3But some plastic materials change over time. S4They crack and frizzle. S5They “weep” out additives. S6They melt into sludge. S7All of which creates huge headaches for institutions, such as museums, trying to preserve culturally important objects. S8The variety of plastic objects at risk is dizzying: early radios, avant-garde sculptures, celluloid animation stills from Disney films, the first artificial heart.
P2 S1Certain artifacts are especially vulnerable because some pioneers in plastic art didn’t always know how to mix ingredients properly, says Thea van Oosten, a polymer chemist who, until retiring a few years ago, worked for decades at the Cultural Heritage Agency of the Netherlands. S2“It’s like baking a cake: If you don’t have exact amounts, it goes wrong,” she says. S3“The object you make is already a time bomb.”
P3 S1And sometimes, it’s not the artist’s fault. S2In the 1960s, the Italian artist Piero Gilardi began to create hundreds of bright, colorful foam pieces. S3Those pieces included small beds of roses and other items as well as a few dozen “nature carpets” — large rectangles decorated with foam pumpkins, cabbages, and watermelons. S4He wanted viewers to walk around on the carpets—which meant they had to be durable.
P4 S1Unfortunately, the polyurethane foam he used is inherently unstable. S2It’s especially vulnerable to light damage, and by the mid-1990s, Gilardi’s pumpkins, roses, and other figures were splitting and crumbling. Museums locked some of them away in the dark.
P5 S1So van Oosten and her colleagues worked to preserve Gilardi’s sculptures. S2They infused some with stabilizing and consolidating chemicals. S3Van Oosten calls those chemicals “sunscreens” because their goal was to prevent further light damage and rebuild worn polymer fibers. S4She is proud that several sculptures have even gone on display again, albeit sometimes beneath protective cases.
P6 S1Despite success stories like van Oosten’s, preservation of plastics will likely get harder. S2Old objects continue to deteriorate. Worse, biodegradable plastics, designed to disintegrate, are increasingly common.
P7 S1And more is at stake here than individual objects. S2Joana Lia Ferreira, an assistant professor of conservation and restoration at the NOVA School of Science and Technology, notes that archaeologists first defined the great material ages of human history—Stone Age, Iron Age, and so on—after examining artifacts in museums. S3We now live in an age of plastic, she says, “and what we decide to collect today, what we decide to preserve...will have a strong impact on how in the future we’ll be seen.”

Q21细节理解题
答案 A

According to Paragraph 1, museums are faced with difficulties in

  • Amaintaining their plastic items.
  • Bobtaining durable plastic artifacts.
  • Chandling outdated plastic exhibits.
  • Dclassifying their plastic collections.
关键词题干关键词 museums / difficulties；原文对应为 museums / huge headaches / trying to preserve / plastic objects at risk。
定位按 Paragraph 1 定位到后半段：前面先反转“塑料很耐用”的常识，随后列出塑料材料会开裂、渗出添加剂、融成污泥，最后落到 museums 的保存困境。
证据原文说这些变化给博物馆等机构造成 huge headaches，因为它们要 preserve culturally important objects；后文又说 plastic objects at risk 种类很多。
替换maintaining their plastic items ≈ trying to preserve culturally important plastic objects；difficulties ≈ huge headaches。

干扰项反证

[B]原文说塑料藏品本身存在保存风险，不是博物馆难以“获得”耐用塑料文物。
[C]outdated 是“过时展品”，原文说 culturally important objects，不是过时。
[D]首段没有谈分类 plastic collections，分类不是困难来源。
Q22原因细节题
答案 C

Van Oosten believes that certain plastic objects are

  • Aimmune to decay.
  • Bimproperly shaped.
  • Cinherently flawed.
  • Dcomplex in structure.
关键词关键词 Van Oosten / certain plastic objects；原文对应为 Certain artifacts / vulnerable / mix ingredients properly / time bomb。
定位人名 Van Oosten 是强定位词，直接定位 P2；该段解释某些塑料艺术品为什么特别脆弱。
证据早期塑料艺术先驱不总是知道如何正确混合成分；配方不精确就会出错，做出的物品已经是 time bomb。
替换inherently flawed ≈ 配方一开始就有问题、对象本身已成 time bomb。

干扰项反证

[A]immune to decay 与 vulnerable、time bomb 方向相反。
[B]improperly shaped 把问题歪曲成形状错误；原文讲成分配比。
[D]complex in structure 原文没有说结构复杂导致衰败。
Q23目的细节题
答案 D

Museums stopped exhibiting some of Gilardi’s artworks to

  • Akeep them from hurting visitors.
  • Bduplicate them for future display.
  • Chave their ingredients analyzed.
  • Dprevent them from further damage.
关键词关键词 stopped exhibiting / Gilardi’s artworks；原文对应为 Museums locked some of them away in the dark / vulnerable to light damage。
定位Gilardi 定位到 P3-P5；停止展出对应 P4 的 locked away in the dark。
证据作品对光照损伤特别脆弱，并已开裂碎裂；博物馆把部分作品锁在黑暗处，合理目的就是防止继续受损。
替换stopped exhibiting ≈ locked away；prevent further damage ≈ avoid light damage / splitting and crumbling。

干扰项反证

[A]作品可被踩踏并不等于会伤害观众，原文没有游客安全问题。
[B]原文没有复制作品。
[C]配方问题在 P2，Gilardi 作品此处是保护处理，不是成分分析。

Q25推断题
答案 B

In Ferreira’s opinion, preservation of plastic artifacts

  • Awill inspire future scientific research.
  • Bhas profound historical significance.
  • Cwill help us separate the material ages.
  • Dhas an impact on today’s cultural life.
关键词关键词 Ferreira / preservation of plastic artifacts；原文对应为 material ages of human history / how in the future we’ll be seen。
定位人名 Ferreira 定位 P7；该段不是谈单件藏品，而是把塑料保存提升到人类历史材料时代的层面。

替换profound historical significance ≈ material ages of human history + how in the future we’ll be seen。

[A]future research 未被提及。
[C]原文说材料时代是考古学家定义历史的方式，不是保存塑料帮助我们“分离”材料时代。
[D]today’s cultural life 把重点放在当下生活，原文重点是未来历史视角。

P1 S1As the latest crop of students pen their undergraduate application form and weigh up their options, it may be worth considering just how the point, purpose and value of a degree has changed and what Generation Z need to consider as they start the third stage of their educational journey.
P2 S1Millennials were told that if you did well in school, got a decent degree, you would be set up for life. S2But that promise has been found wanting. S3As degrees became universal, they became devalued. Education was no longer a secure route of social mobility. Today, 28 per cent of graduates in the UK are in non-graduate roles, a percentage which is double the average among the OECD countries.
P3 S1This is not to say that there is no point in getting a degree, but rather stress that a degree is not for everyone, that the switch from classroom to lecture hall is not an inevitable one and that other options are available.
P4 S1Thankfully, there are signs that this is already happening, with Generation Z seeking to learn from their millennial predecessors, even if parents and teachers tend to be still set in the degree mindset. S2Employers have long seen the advantages of hiring school leavers who often prove themselves to be more committed and loyal employees than graduates. Many too are seeing the advantages of scrapping a degree requirement for certain roles.
P5 S1For those for whom a degree is the desired route, consider that this may well be the first of many. S2In this age of generalists, it pays to have specific knowledge or skills. Postgraduates now earn 40 per cent more than graduates. When more and more of us have a degree, it makes sense to have two.
P6 S1It is unlikely that Generation Z will be done with education at 18 or 21; they will need to be constantly up-skilling throughout their career to stay employable. S2It has been estimated that this generation, due to the pressures of technology, the wish for personal fulfillment and desire for diversity, will work for 17 different employers over the course of their working life and have five different careers. S3Education, and not just knowledge gained on campus, will be a core part of Generation Z’s career trajectory.
P7 S1Older generations often talk about their degree in the present and personal tense: ‘I am a geographer’ or ‘I am a classicist’. S2Their sons or daughters would never say such a thing; it’s as if they already know that their degree won’t define them in the same way.

完整原文｜定位词与证据原位标注

彩色加粗 marker = 题干关键词或原文同义定位表达；粗下划线 + Q 标签 = 作答证据。

P1 S1Enlightening, challenging, stimulating, fun. These were some of the words that Nature readers used to describe their experiences of art-science collaborations in a series of articles on partnerships between artists and researchers. S2Nearly 40% of the roughly 350 people who responded to an accompanying poll said they had collaborated with artists; and almost all said they would consider doing so in future.
P2 S1Such an encouraging result is not surprising. S2Scientists are increasingly seeking out visual artists to help them communicate their work to new audiences. “Artists help scientists reach a broader audience and make emotional connections that enhance learning,” one respondent said.
P3 S1One example of how artists and scientists have together rocked the senses came last month when the Sydney Symphony Orchestra performed a reworked version of Antonio Vivaldi’s The Four Seasons. S2They reimagined the 300-year-old score by injecting the latest climate prediction data for each season—provided by Monash University’s Climate Change Communication Research Hub. The performance was a creative call to action ahead of November’s United Nations Climate Change Conference in Glasgow, UK.
P4 S1But a genuine partnership must be a two-way street. S2Fewer artists than scientists responded to the Nature poll; however, several respondents noted that artists do not simply assist scientists with their communication requirements. Nor should their work be considered only as an object of study. S3The alliances are most valuable when scientists and artists have a shared stake in a project, are able to jointly design it and can critique each other’s work. S4Such an approach can both prompt new research as well as result in powerful art.
P5 S1More than half a century ago, the Massachusetts Institute of Technology opened its Center for Advanced Visual Studies (CAVS) to explore the role of technology in culture. S2The founders deliberately focused their projects around light—hence the ‘visual studies’ in the name. S3Light was something that both artists and scientists had an interest in, and therefore could form the basis of collaboration. As science and technology progressed, and divided into more sub-disciplines, the centre was simultaneously looking to a time when leading researchers could also be artists, writers and poets, and vice versa.
P6 S1Nature’s poll findings suggest that this trend is as strong as ever, but, to make a collaboration work, both sides need to invest time, and embrace surprise and challenge. S2The reach of art-science tie-ups needs to go beyond the necessary purpose of research communication, and participants must not fall into the trap of stereotyping each other. S3Artists and scientists alike are immersed in discovery and invention, and challenge and critique are core to both, too.

P1 S1The personal grievance provisions of New Zealand’s Employment Relations Act 2000 (ERA) prevent an employer from firing an employee without good cause. S2Instead, dismissals must be justified. S3Employers must both show cause and act in a procedurally fair way.
P2 S1Personal grievance procedures were designed to guard the jobs of ordinary workers from “unjustified dismissals”. S2The premise was that the common law of contract lacked sufficient safeguards for workers against arbitrary conduct by management. S3Long gone are the days when a boss could simply give an employee contractual notice.
P3 S1But these provisions create difficulties for businesses when applied to highly paid managers and executives. S2As countless boards and business owners will attest, constraining firms from firing poorly performing, high-earning managers is a handbrake on boosting productivity and overall performance. S3The difference between C-grade and A-grade managers may very well be the difference between business success or failure. S4Between preserving the jobs of ordinary workers or losing them. S5Yet mediocrity is no longer enough to justify a dismissal.
P4 S1Consequently—and paradoxically—laws introduced to protect the jobs of ordinary workers may be placing those jobs at risk.
P5 S1If not placing jobs at risk, to the extent employment protection laws constrain business owners from dismissing underperforming managers, those laws act as a constraint on firm productivity and therefore on workers’ wages. S2Indeed, in “An International Perspective on New Zealand’s Productivity Paradox” (2014), the Productivity Commission singled out the low quality of managerial capabilities as a cause of the country’s poor productivity growth record.
P6 S1Nor are highly paid managers themselves immune from the harm caused by the ERA’s unjustified dismissal procedures. S2Because employment protection laws make it costlier to fire an employee, employers are more cautious about hiring new staff. S3This makes it harder for the marginal manager to gain employment. S4And firms pay staff less because firms carry the burden of the employment arrangement going wrong.
P7 S1Society also suffers from excessive employment protections. S2Stringent job dismissal regulations adversely affect productivity growth and hamper both prosperity and overall well-being.
P8 S1Across the Tasman Sea, Australia deals with the unjustified dismissal paradox by excluding employees earning above a specified “high-income threshold” from the protection of its unfair dismissal laws. S2In New Zealand, a 2016 private members’ Bill tried to permit firms and high-income employees to contract out of the unjustified dismissal regime. S3However, the mechanisms proposed were unwieldy and the Bill was voted down following the change in government later that year.
